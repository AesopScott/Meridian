# Meridian Prime Directives

Meridian Prime Directives are the load-bearing ideas that make Meridian different.

They are not ordinary features. They are the architectural and product claims the rest of Meridian should keep proving.

## 1. Logic, Not Rules

Meridian should be governed by logic, not brittle rule piles.

Rules can exist, but they should be rare, explicit, and foundational. Prime should reason from mission, context, risk, memory, proof, and user intent rather than blindly following a growing list of procedural commands.

The goal is adaptable judgment with inspectable reasoning, not rigid automation.

## 2. Prime As The Reference Line

Prime is Meridian's orchestrator/local brain.

Like the Prime Meridian, Prime gives the system a reference point. It establishes position, sets bearing, coordinates harnesses, and decides what should move next.

## 3. Mission-First Boot

Prime wakes by reading `MISSION.md` before acting.

The mission file is the launch protocol and authority chain for the current run. It tells Prime what to follow, which harnesses to call, in what order, and which immutable rules apply.

## 4. Progress Intention

Prime states meaningful work as intention before execution.

The wake brief should not be a loading transcript. It should say what Prime intends to work on, why it is prioritized, what stage it is in, how many lanes or sessions it expects to use, and where Scott may modify or gate the work.

## 5. Risk-Tiered Dual-Lane Cognition

Risk-tiered dual-lane cognition is Meridian's decision engine.

Prime should not rely on one model pass for meaningful decisions. As risk increases, Prime can move from deterministic logic, to one model lane, to two independent cognition lanes, to proof-backed validation, to a human gate.

## 6. Dynamic Risk Tiering

Prime can change risk tiers as work evolves.

Changing risk tier changes the decision process more deeply than changing models. It changes scrutiny, lane count, proof requirements, and human gating.

## 7. Persistent Prime Context

Prime's effective memory is not bounded by any one model context window.

Worker and model sessions can fill up, drift, summarize poorly, or end. Prime stays durable by using Echo memory, Atlas knowledge/RAG, focused memory injection, and eventually deeper search.

## 8. Prime-Centric Workspace

Meridian is not a wall of worker sessions.

The interface centers on Prime. Worker sessions become inspectable machinery behind the orchestrator, while Scott works primarily through Prime's conversation, progress intentions, queues, and review gates.

## 9. Orchestrator And Non-Orchestrator Queues

The Orchestrator Queue is where Scott and Prime communicate.

The Non-Orchestrator Queue is where Prime places artifacts, plans, comparisons, proof, worker outputs, and approval items it wants Scott to see or gate.

## 10. Prime Owns Routine Coordination

Scott should not be the review dispatcher.

Prime owns routine builder, reviewer, verifier, retry, rejection, repair, and proof loops. Scott should mostly enter for final plan review, strategy, taste, irreversible actions, public actions, account-risking actions, or explicit takeover.

## 11. Harnesses As Capabilities

Harnesses are Prime's capabilities, not the user's chores.

Relay manages models and sessions. Echo remembers. Beacon reports health. Aegis proves. Bifrost provides the UI bridge. Forge/Vulcan acts through tools. Atlas retrieves knowledge. The harnesses make Prime operational.

## Naming Note

Prime Directives is the current internal name for these load-bearing ideas. If public branding later needs a less pop-culture-coded phrase, use Meridian Pillars as the fallback.

## Short Product Claim

Meridian is a Prime-centered AI command system where persistent memory, dynamic risk-tiered cognition, proof-backed harnesses, and human-gated progress intentions let weak model sessions become reliable coordinated work.
